# NURSING BASICS — CLINICAL KNOWLEDGE FOR NURSING STUDENTS (ENGLISH)

## 1. VITAL SIGNS — MEASUREMENT AND MONITORING

### Blood Pressure (Blutdruck)
Normal values: 120/80 mmHg (systolic/diastolic)
- Hypotension (low blood pressure): below 100/60 mmHg → dizziness, weakness, fainting risk
- Hypertension (high blood pressure): above 140/90 mmHg → headaches, cardiovascular risk
Measurement: Upper arm cuff, patient should rest 5 minutes beforehand
Documentation: Always record value, time, and which arm (left/right)

### Pulse (Puls)
Normal range for adults: 60–100 beats per minute (bpm)
- Bradycardia: below 60 bpm → slow heart rate
- Tachycardia: above 100 bpm → fast heart rate
Measurement: Radial artery (inside of wrist), count for 30 seconds and multiply by 2

### Body Temperature (Körpertemperatur)
Normal range: 36.5°C – 37.5°C (97.7°F – 99.5°F)
- Subfebrile: 37.5°C – 38.0°C
- Fever (Fieber): above 38°C (100.4°F)
- High fever: above 39.5°C (103.1°F)
- Hypothermia: below 35°C (95°F)
Measurement sites: oral, axillary (armpit), rectal, or ear (tympanic)

### Respiratory Rate (Atemfrequenz)
Normal for adults: 12–20 breaths per minute
- Bradypnoea: below 12/min → abnormally slow breathing
- Tachypnoea: above 20/min → abnormally fast breathing
- Dyspnoea: shortness of breath / laboured breathing

### Oxygen Saturation — SpO₂ (Sauerstoffsättigung)
Normal: 95–100%
- Below 90%: Warning sign — assess need for supplemental oxygen
- Measurement: Pulse oximeter clipped to finger

## 2. HYGIENE AND INFECTION CONTROL

### WHO 5 Moments of Hand Hygiene
1. BEFORE patient contact
2. BEFORE an aseptic/clean procedure
3. AFTER contact with body fluids
4. AFTER patient contact
5. AFTER contact with the patient's environment

Hand disinfection technique:
- Apply 3–5 ml alcohol-based hand rub
- Rub for 30 seconds covering: palms, backs of hands, between fingers, thumbs, fingertips

### Personal Protective Equipment (PPE / Schutzausrüstung)
- Disposable gloves: Required whenever contacting body fluids
- Surgical mask (MNS): For droplet infections
- FFP2/FFP3 respirator: For airborne infections (e.g., TB, COVID-19)
- Protective gown (Schutzkittel): For isolation patients
- Eye protection: When splash risk exists

### Types of Isolation
- Contact isolation: For MRSA, Norovirus → gown + gloves
- Droplet isolation: For influenza → surgical mask
- Airborne isolation: For tuberculosis, measles → single room + FFP2 mask

## 3. BASIC NURSING CARE (GRUNDPFLEGE)

### Full Body Wash (Ganzkörperwäsche)
Procedure:
1. Prepare materials: wash bowl, washcloths, towels, clean clothing
2. Preserve dignity: Only expose the area being washed
3. Order: Face → neck → upper body → arms → hands → intimate area → legs → back
4. Change water after intimate area!
5. Skin inspection: Check for pressure sores, redness, wounds
6. Skin care: Apply patient's own moisturizer/lotion
7. Dress patient and position comfortably

### Oral Care (Mundpflege)
Important for: unconscious patients, stroke patients, ventilated patients
Procedure: Clean teeth/dentures, keep mucous membranes moist, apply lip balm

### Pressure Ulcer Prevention (Dekubitusprophylaxe)
Decubitus (pressure ulcer) = skin damage from prolonged pressure
Risk areas: heels, sacrum/tailbone, shoulder blades, back of head, ankles
Prevention:
- Position changes every 2 hours (30° positioning, soft mattress overlays)
- Pressure-relieving equipment: special mattresses, heel protectors
- Regular skin inspection and moisturizing
- Ensure adequate hydration

## 4. MEDICATION ADMINISTRATION (BASICS)

### The 5 Rights of Medication Safety (5-R-Regel)
1. Right Patient — verify identity (check name band!)
2. Right Medication
3. Right Dose
4. Right Time
5. Right Route (oral, IV, SC, IM, etc.)

### Routes of Administration
- Oral (p.o.): By mouth — tablets, capsules, drops
- Sublingual: Under the tongue (e.g., nitroglycerin for angina)
- Rectal: Via rectum (suppositories)
- Subcutaneous (SC): Under the skin (e.g., insulin, heparin)
- Intramuscular (IM): Into the muscle (e.g., vaccines)
- Intravenous (IV): Directly into a vein (infusions, fast action)
- Transdermal: Through the skin (patches)
- Inhalation: Via airways (asthma inhalers)

### Documentation of Medication
EVERY medication given must be documented immediately:
- Drug name, dose, time, route of administration
- Name/initials of the administering nurse
- Special notes (e.g., patient vomited, side effects observed)

## 5. NURSING DOCUMENTATION (PFLEGEDOKUMENTATION)

### What Gets Documented?
- Vital signs (blood pressure, pulse, temperature, respiration, SpO₂)
- Nursing care activities (hygiene, positioning, wound dressing changes)
- Medications administered
- Fluid balance (intake and output)
- Bowel and bladder function
- Pain assessment (NRS scale: 0–10; 0 = no pain, 10 = worst possible pain)
- Patient observations and changes in condition
- Doctor's orders and their execution

### Principles of Good Documentation
1. Timely: Document immediately after performing care
2. Legible: Clear and readable handwriting (or digital entry)
3. Accurate: Cross out errors with one line, sign, and correct (never use correction fluid)
4. Complete: No gaps or missing information
5. Objective: Record observations, not assumptions
6. Confidential: Patient records = sensitive data → medical confidentiality (Schweigepflicht) applies

## 6. NUTRITION AND FLUIDS

### Recognizing Malnutrition (Mangelernährung)
- Weight loss > 5% in 3 months or > 10% in 6 months
- BMI below 18.5 kg/m²
- Screening tools: MNA (Mini Nutritional Assessment) or NRS 2002

### Dysphagia (Schluckstörung — Swallowing Disorder)
Common after: Stroke, neurological conditions, dementia
Symptoms: Coughing during meals, choking, refusing food, weight loss
Management: Speech therapy (Logopädie), modified texture diet, thickened fluids

### Fluid Balance
Normal daily fluid intake: 1.5 – 2.5 litres
Intake: Drinking fluids + IV infusions + fluids in food
Output: Urine + stool + perspiration + respiration (~500 ml/day)
Balance = Intake minus Output (should be approximately zero)

## 7. RECOGNIZING EMERGENCY SITUATIONS

### Cardiac Arrest (Herzkreislaufstillstand)
Signs: Unconsciousness, no normal breathing, no pulse
Immediate action: Call emergency (112 or internal code), begin BLS/CPR
CPR ratio: 30 chest compressions : 2 rescue breaths
Rate: 100–120 compressions/min, depth: 5–6 cm

### Stroke (Schlaganfall) — FAST Assessment
F — Face: Is one side of the face drooping?
A — Arms: Can the patient raise both arms equally?
S — Speech: Is speech slurred or confused?
T — Time: Call 112 immediately! Every minute counts!

### Hypoglycaemia (Unterzuckerung — Low Blood Sugar)
Blood glucose below 70 mg/dl (3.9 mmol/l)
Symptoms: Trembling, sweating, pallor, confusion, possible unconsciousness
Management:
- If conscious: Give glucose tablets, fruit juice, or sugary drink
- If unconscious: Do NOT give anything orally — call emergency team

### Anaphylaxis (Severe Allergic Reaction)
Symptoms: Skin rash/urticaria, breathing difficulty, blood pressure drop, loss of consciousness
Immediate action: Emergency call, adrenaline (EpiPen if available), oxygen, appropriate positioning

## 8. COMMUNICATION WITH PATIENTS

### Therapeutic Communication Principles
- Active listening: Eye contact, nodding, don't interrupt
- Open-ended questions: "How are you feeling?" not "Are you okay?"
- Show empathy: "I understand this must be difficult for you."
- Biographical work: Get to know the patient's life story (especially in elderly care)

### Communication with Dementia Patients
- Use simple, short sentences
- Speak slowly, maintain eye contact
- Do NOT force reality orientation
- Validation: Take the patient's feelings seriously
- Watch for non-verbal cues (facial expressions, body language)

### Breaking Bad News
- Create a quiet, private environment
- Speak clearly and avoid medical jargon
- Allow time for reactions
- Offer support (chaplain, psychologist, social worker)

## 9. NURSING ETHICS AND PATIENT RIGHTS

### Core Ethical Principles in Nursing
1. Autonomy: Patient's right to make their own decisions
2. Beneficence: Acting in the patient's best interest
3. Non-maleficence: Do no harm
4. Justice: Fair, equal treatment for all patients

### Patient Rights in Germany
- Right to information about diagnosis and treatment
- Right to consent or refuse treatment (Einwilligung)
- Right to confidentiality (Schweigepflicht)
- Right to dignity and respect
- Right to pain management
- Right to involve family members (with patient consent)

### Medical Confidentiality (Schweigepflicht)
All patient information is strictly confidential.
Information may only be shared with:
- Other members of the direct care team
- Other healthcare providers involved in care (with patient consent)
- Emergency services when patient safety is at risk
Violation of Schweigepflicht is a criminal offense in Germany.
